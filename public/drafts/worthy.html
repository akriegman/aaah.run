worthy

31 May 2026

If you ever feel like you’re unworthy or invalid, it’s important to remember that you are here for a reason. But don’t get confused: some people will tell you that everyone is worthy, that we all have intrinsic value. This is completely backwards. We are not worthy because we are here, we’re here because we are worthy. In particular, the reason you’re here is that your parents fucked. And you’re made out of the same stuff they’re made out of. That’s the reason that you can be expected to be worthy.

But we’re not clones of our parents, we’re a random mixture of them plus mutations. So just because they made it doesn’t mean there’s any reason to believe that you will, right? This is a misunderstanding. We are randomized, but the way and degree to which we are randomized is not random, and is not completely determined by the infeasibility of less random reproduction. So not only are you made from worthy source material, but you are also a worthy experiment.

This isn’t completely clear though. We have several traits which do seem to just be consequences of chemistry. For instance, the conversion of ATP to energy is only 60% efficient, so nearly half of the energy we use is dissipated as heat, likely much more due to other inefficiencies in our body. I suppose this could be an adaptation to warm our bodies… but it seems more likely that there just wasn’t a better chemical for transferring energy around the body. Another example is drugs. Usually when something is bad for us we adapt to not enjoy it. But because we are chemical beings there will always be chemicals that can hack us.

But there is reason to believe that our reproduction does not fall in this category. For one thing, reproductive processes vary greatly across the tree of life. Asexual reproduction and non-gendered sexual reproductive and almost every variation you can think of have all been tried, and our particular method of gendered sexual reproduction at this particular mutation rate is the one that has produced the intelligence explosion. Perhaps some trisexual or polysexual form of reproduction would be even more adaptive and we just haven’t evolved it yet, but it appears as though our reproduction is mostly determined by optimizing adaptivity and not by saturating feasibility constraints.

We have a gene which acts as a checksum, detecting errors in our genome and correcting them. The more copies you have of this gene, the less likely you are to get cancer. Humans have 2 or 3 copies of this gene. Elephants have 40. Elephants need to have 40 because otherwise with how large they are they would be entirely cancer. So why don’t humans have more? Sounds like an unabated good so far right? Have we just not managed to evolve in that direction yet, or is there a downside to this gene? The fact that we have not adapted to lower our mutation rate as much as we could suggests that our mutation rate was chosen “deliberately”1.

So all I ask is that you give credit where credit is due. Think of all the yearning and flirting and overthinking and games and gossip that your ancestors had to do to make you who you are. It’s disrespectful to take your worthiness for granted.

  1. In quotes because I’m personifying evolution. Evolution does not choose things, it tries things and the things that work stick around. I’d argue that calling this “deliberate” is more than a metaphor though: my theory is that internally we follow the same process of generation and selection, so “deliberate” actions are also the result of an evolutionary process, just on a much shorter timescale and happening mostly inside your brain so that we only see the output after several rounds of selection.  ↩︎
